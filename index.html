The Darby Family

Eleven generations in America. A family that started in colonial Massachusetts around 1650, pushed through Connecticut and New York, and settled in Iowa by 1864. The name itself goes back to 13th-century Lincolnshire.

A Timeline

Key dates in the family record, from the earliest English documentation to Iowa.

c. 1200, Lincolnshire, England
The Name Appears in Lincolnshire
The earliest recorded Darby family: a pedigree noting that Nicholas Tamworth of Tamworth married Jane, daughter of Darby, of Leverton, Lincolnshire, around 1200. The name derives from Old Norse djur-byr (deer settlement), the place name for Derby, Derbyshire, recorded in the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle as "Doeraby" in 917 AD.
1346 to 1620, Lincolnshire
Darby Hall, Leake and Leverton
Ralph de Darby of Bennington (1346), Thomas Darby (1346, 1353), Robert Darby (1357), Roger Darby of Leverton, Ralph Darby of Leake (1517), Thomas Darby of Bennington (1536 to 1544). The family held Darby Hall, a large property in Leake and Leverton, Lincolnshire, described as "the residence of the family of that name who were considerable proprietors in this and the adjoining parishes for several centuries." Memorials of the Darby and Gilbert families survive in the chancel floor at Leake church.
1623, London and New England
First Darby in American Colonial Records
William Darby of Dorchester, England petitioned the Council for New England on February 18, 1623, for a plantation at Cape Ann, which eventually led to the settlement of Naumkeag (now Salem, Massachusetts). William Darby also appears among Representatives to the General Court in England on October 12, 1629, placing a Darby at the administrative founding moment of Massachusetts Bay Colony.
1650, Surrey, England
Agnes Derby's Will Names Edward Derby in New England
Agnes Derby of Bisley, Surrey made a will on May 20, 1650 (proved June 1, 1650), leaving money to Henry Collier "He to pay Edward Derby, in New England, ten pounds when he shall come and demand the same." This is the strongest English document connecting your direct ancestor Edward¹ Darby to a specific family and location. Bisley sits about 30 miles southwest of London.
January 25, 1658/59, Boston
Edward Derby Marries Susanna Hooke
Boston vital records confirm: "Edward Derby was married to Susanna Hook, January 25, 1659, by Richard Bellingham, Deputy Governor." Bellingham was a sitting or former Governor of Massachusetts Bay Colony. Edward later settled in Braintree, Massachusetts, where daughters Mary (born December 29, 1660) and Priscilla (born June 20, 1672) are recorded.
1702, Dunstable, Massachusetts
James² Darby Born
James² Darby was born April 30, 1702 in Dunstable, Essex (now Middlesex), Massachusetts. He later settled at Mortlake Society, Canterbury, Connecticut, one of the more active Separatist/Baptist communities in colonial New England. He married Eleanor Shepherd, connecting the Darby line to Puritan immigrants Ralph Shepard (London, 1635) and John Whitney (London and Isleworth, 1635).
1731 to 1813, Canterbury, CT / Cortland, NY
Eleazor³ Darby Lives Through the Revolution
Eleazor³ was born July 6, 1731 in Canterbury, Connecticut and was 44 to 52 years old during the Revolutionary War. He lived in a strongly Patriot community. Connecticut was known as "The Provisions State." He died January 31, 1813 in Cortland, New York, aged 81. Buried at Cortland Old Burial Ground. His Revolutionary War service is an open research question and the most likely path to SAR eligibility.
1796 to 1806, Homer, New York
Joseph⁴ Marries Anna Grow
Joseph⁴ Darby married Anna Grow of Hampton, Connecticut in 1796. Anna (b. February 15, 1777) was the daughter of Thomas Grow, whose family had settled on Grow Hill in Hampton in 1723. She died June 30, 1806, aged 29, leaving three children: Delia, Lyman, and Thomas (your ancestor). Joseph later married Asenath Tyler in 1807. Their descendants spread throughout Cortland County, NY across the 19th century.
1864, Cortland, NY to Clarksville, Iowa
Thomas DeWight⁶ Moves to Iowa
Thomas DeWight⁶ Darby (b. November 23, 1842, Cortland, NY) married Frances Elizabeth Bement in 1864 and moved to Clarksville, Iowa, the first Darby in Iowa. He died August 30, 1900 and is buried at Lynwood Cemetery, Clarksville. His son Lucius Edwin⁷ (b. 1868) became a Plymouth Brethren lay minister and farmer in Clarksville, keeping the family in Butler County, Iowa for the next several generations.
1896 to 1985, Clarksville and Nashua, Iowa
Clarence Arthur⁸, Baptist Farmer
Clarence Arthur⁸ (b. July 7, 1896) married Katie Sutter on May 10, 1917 in Charles City, Iowa. Katie was originally named Katrina Maria but had no birth certificate (born at home) and chose the name "Katie Darby," what she had always been called. Baptist farmer. Five children. Died August 28, 1985 at Oak Hill Cemetery, Nashua, Iowa.
September 12, 1987, Waterloo, Iowa
Jacob Richard¹¹ Darby
The 11th American generation of the family, and the 13th or 14th from the English roots in Lincolnshire and Surrey. Six documented family lines: Darby, Grow, Shepherd, Whitney, Hooke, and Podhaski from the Polish highlands. Current residence: West Branch, Iowa.

The Connected Lines

Every name in this tree traces to a different place. The Darby name is the spine, but the branches run through Connecticut, London, Bristol, Hampton, and the Polish highlands.

Darby / Derby

Lincolnshire, England to Boston, MA to Iowa (c.1200 to present)
The patriline. Name from Old Norse djur-byr (deer settlement). First recorded c.1200 in Lincolnshire. First American: Edward Derby, Boston 1650s. Held Darby Hall in Leake and Leverton, Lincolnshire, for several centuries. Coat of arms: argent, a stag's head caboshed gules, attired or, between three roundels azure.
Direct Patriline

Grow / Groh

Germanic/Low Countries to Ipswich MA to Hampton CT (c.1700 to 1800s)
Anna (Grow) Darby, wife of Joseph⁴ (b. 1777, Hampton CT), was the daughter of Thomas Grow. The founding Grow settler came from Ipswich, MA in 1723 and settled on Grow Hill in Hampton, CT. Grow/Groh is Germanic in origin. A collateral descendant, Galusha Aaron Grow, served as 24th Speaker of the U.S. House of Representatives from 1861 to 1863 and passed the Homestead Act of 1862.
Joseph⁴'s First Wife

Shepherd / Shepard

Limehouse, Stepney, London to Dedham MA (1606 to present)
Eleanor Shepherd, wife of James² Darby, was the daughter of Samuel Shepherd and Eleanor Whitney. The line traces to Deacon Ralph Shepard (b. 1606, Limehouse, Stepney, London), a Puritan tailor who was brought before the Court of High Commissions as a nonconformist in 1634 and left England June 30, 1635 aboard the ship Abigail. He co-founded Dedham, Massachusetts. A collateral descendant: Eli Whitney, inventor of the cotton gin.
James²'s Wife's Line

Whitney

Isleworth-on-Thames, London to Watertown MA (c.1589 to present)
Eleanor Whitney (mother of Eleanor Shepherd, James²'s wife) descends from John Whitney Sr., a Merchant Tailor's Guild member born c.1589 at Isleworth, 8 miles from Westminster. He arrived in Watertown, Massachusetts in July 1635 on the ship Elizabeth and Ann. Freeman of Massachusetts Bay Colony (1636), Selectman 1638 to 1655, Town Clerk 1655. Full genealogy: Pierce, 1895 (free at Internet Archive).
Great Migration 1635

Hooke

Bristol, Gloucestershire, England to Salisbury MA (c.1610 to 1660s)
Susanna Hooke, wife of Edward¹ Darby, likely connected to the Hooke family of Bristol. Humphrey Hooke of Bristol (c.1580s to 1658) was a Member of Parliament and prominent Bristol merchant. His son William Hooke (c.1612 to 1652) emigrated to Salisbury, Massachusetts and became Governor of New Somerseshire. Published study: Frederick W. Todd, Humphrey Hooke of Bristol and His Family and Descendants.
Edward¹'s Wife's Line

Podhaski

Podhale Region, Southern Poland to Waterloo, Iowa
Ella Podhaski, wife of Erwin Clarence⁹ Darby (married February 20, 1947, Waterloo, Iowa), carries a Polish toponymic surname. Podhaski means "from the Podhale," the highland plateau at the foot of the Tatra Mountains in southern Poland, near Krakow. Her family likely arrived in Iowa during the Polish immigration wave of the 1880s to 1910s. The village of origin in Poland is an open research question.
Erwin⁹'s Wife's Line

Tyler

Connecticut (colonial era) to Cortland, NY
Asenath Tyler, Joseph⁴ Darby's second wife (married September 20, 1807, Cortland, NY), was born in Connecticut. Tyler is an English occupational surname (tile-maker). Asenath's children with Joseph⁴ include Ann, Joseph Jr., Lucius Tyler, Chauncey (Baptist minister), Asenath, and Edwin, founding the other major Darby branches in Cortland County, New York.
Joseph⁴'s Second Wife

Salisbury

Connecticut (colonial era) to Cortland, NY
Cynthia Salisbury, wife of Lyman⁵ Darby (married December 12, 1823, Cortland, NY), is connected through your ancestor Thomas⁵'s brother. The family record confirms the Salisbury line as one of the SAR-eligible threads. A Captain Abraham Salisbury is documented in Connecticut SAR records assisting Seth Warner's militia at Lake Champlain in July 1777. Cynthia's parents are a research gap.
Lyman⁵'s Wife's Line

Origins in England

Several branches of this family left documented records in England before emigrating. The Darby name appears in Lincolnshire as early as 1200. Other lines came through London, Bristol, and Surrey.

Darby Hall, Leake and Leverton, Lincolnshire

c. 1200 to c. 1700

Darby Hall stood as the family seat in Leake (partly Leverton), Lincolnshire for several centuries. The family were "considerable proprietors in this and the adjoining parishes," allied by marriage to the Dymocke family. Documented members include Ralph Darby (1346), Thomas Darby (1346, 1353), Robert (1357), Roger of Leverton, Ralph of Leake (1517), Thomas of Bennington (1536 to 1544), William (1571), Thomas (1597 to 1659), Dymoke Darby (died Leake 1701). Memorials to the Darby and Gilbert families survive in the Leake church chancel floor.

Ralph Shepard, Stepney and Limehouse, London

1606 to 1635 emigration

Ralph Shepard was born c.1606 in the Limehouse district of Stepney, Middlesex, a neighborhood named for its lime kilns along the Thames. A tailor by profession, he was brought before the Court of High Commissions as a religious nonconformist on April 24, 1634. He married Thankslorde Perkins at St. Bride Fleet Street, London on May 21, 1632, then left England June 30, 1635 aboard the ship Abigail. He helped found Dedham, Massachusetts, signing the Dedham Covenant. He died 1693 in Malden, Massachusetts, aged about 87.

John Whitney, Isleworth-on-Thames and Bowe Lane, London

c.1589 to 1635 emigration

John Whitney Sr. was born c.1589 and lived at Isleworth-on-Thames, 8 miles upstream from Westminster, where his eldest children were christened. He later lived on Bowe Lane in the City of London, near St. Paul's Cathedral. A member of the Merchant Tailors' Company, he sailed in April 1635 and arrived in Watertown, Massachusetts in July 1635 on the ship Elizabeth and Ann. He was granted 16 acres on arrival and 198 more by 1642. Town Clerk of Watertown until his death June 1, 1673. Full genealogy: Pierce, 1895 (free at Internet Archive).

Edward Derby, Bisley, Surrey

c.1625, named in 1650 English will

Agnes Derby of Bisley, Surrey made a will on May 20, 1650 (proved June 1, 1650) leaving money to Henry Collier, "He to pay Edward Derby, in New England, ten pounds when he shall come and demand the same." Bisley is about 30 miles southwest of London. This is the strongest English record connecting your direct ancestor Edward¹ Darby to a specific place. He married Susanna Hooke in Boston on January 25, 1658/59 and settled in Braintree, Massachusetts.

Humphrey Hooke MP, Bristol, Gloucestershire

c.1580s to 1658

Humphrey Hooke of Bristol was a Member of Parliament and prominent Bristol merchant. His son William Hooke (c.1612 to 1652) emigrated to Salisbury, Massachusetts and became Governor of New Somerseshire. William died in 1652, buried at St. Stephens, Bristol. Humphrey's will dated June 25, 1658 names grandsons William, Jacob, and Josias, calling two of them "the most stubborn and unruly boys." Susanna Hooke who married Edward Derby in Boston five months later is the probable granddaughter or niece. See: Frederick W. Todd, Humphrey Hooke of Bristol and His Family and Descendants.

Podhaski Family, Podhale Region, Southern Poland

c.1880s to 1910s immigration to Iowa

Ella Podhaski's surname is a Polish topographic name meaning "from the Podhale," the highland plateau at the foot of the Tatra Mountains in southern Poland, near Krakow, in the Malopolska region. Polish immigration to the American Midwest concentrated in the 1880s to 1910s. Finding Ella's parents' village of origin would connect this line to Central European records and open a separate branch of research.

Notable Collateral Relatives

Collateral, Grow Line
Galusha Aaron Grow
b. August 31, 1823, Eastford, CT. d. March 31, 1907.
24th Speaker of the U.S. House of Representatives (1861 to 1863). He presided during the early years of the Civil War and championed the Homestead Act of 1862. Born on Grow Hill in Hampton, Connecticut, the same hill Thomas Grow (Anna Grow Darby's father) settled in 1723. He and Anna Grow share the same Thomas Grow as their ancestor.
Collateral, Shepard/Whitney Line
Eli Whitney
b. December 8, 1765, Westborough, MA. d. January 8, 1825.
Inventor of the cotton gin (1793) and an early developer of interchangeable parts. Published genealogical records confirm Eli Whitney descends from Ralph Shepard of Limehouse, Stepney, London (1635), the same Ralph Shepard whose line runs through Eleanor Shepherd into James² Darby. Eli Whitney is a distant collateral relative through that Shepard line.
Collateral, Edwin T.⁶ branch
Dr. Edwin T.⁶ Darby
b. August 21, 1845. Burial: Homer Old Cemetery.
President of the American Dental Association, 1893. Practiced in Lansdowne, Pennsylvania and had a summer home on Skaneateles Lake. Invented the Darby Tooth Scaler and Darby Tooth Powder, both still in use. Son of Chauncey⁵ Darby, who was Joseph⁴'s son by his second wife Asenath Tyler, placing this in a collateral branch of your tree.
Collateral, Chollar/Darby Pottery
Chollar and Darby Pottery
Homer and Cortland, New York, c.1839 to 1844
Joseph Jr.⁵ Darby, Thomas⁵'s half-brother, went into the pottery business with Thomas Chollar in 1839. Stoneware marked "Chollar and Darby" with Homer NY or Cortland NY still turns up at antique auctions. Joseph ran the Homer location until around 1839, then sold to Madison Woodruff. All three names are traceable in American antique ceramics.

Sons of the American Revolution

The family record explicitly confirms eligibility: "Chester Chauncey Darby was eligible for membership in the Sons of the American Revolution through the Darby, Grow and Salisbury lines." Chester's eligibility flows through the same Joseph⁴ Darby as your direct line. America's 250th anniversary is July 4, 2026.

The Grow Line

Thomas Grow of Hampton, CT (Anna Grow's father, b. c.1740 to 1750) was 25 to 45 during the Revolution. Hampton men marched through the night to respond to Lexington in April 1775. Town records note "all able-bodied men were away in their country's service." The first step: search the DAR GRS free ancestor database at dar.org for "Grow" in Connecticut.

What We Still Need to Find

These are the gaps in the record, in rough order of how much each one would extend what we know.

1

Thomas Grow's Parents, Hampton CT Records

Finding Thomas Grow's father closes the SAR Grow line. Search the Hampton CT Barbour Collection (FamilySearch, free), Hampton Town Records 1786 to 1856, Hampton First Congregational Church Records 1723 to 1858, and Hampton Baptist Church 1770 to 1853, both at FamilySearch Digital Library.

2

Eleazor³ Darby's Revolutionary War Service

Connecticut State Library holds 37 volumes of Revolutionary War records (1763 to 1789). Search Canterbury CT town records, militia rosters, and oath lists for "Eleazor" or "Eleazer Darby." Also worth checking: Jesse Darby, Eleazor's brother, who was 38 to 45 during the Revolution and may also have service records.

3

Edward¹ Darby's English Origin, Ship Passenger Records

Agnes Derby's 1650 will (Bisley, Surrey) names "Edward Derby, in New England." Search Surrey and Bisley parish records for Derby families c.1620 to 1650, London parish records for Stepney, Southwark, or Surrey, and Port of London passenger lists 1640 to 1658. If Edward arrived in 1630 per one early record, check the 1630 Winthrop Fleet passenger lists.

4

Ella Podhaski's Polish Village of Origin

Iowa State Historical Society records, Waterloo-area Catholic and Orthodox parish records from the 1880s to 1920s, and Polish immigration records at Ellis Island (free at Ancestry and FamilySearch). The Podhale region in southern Poland includes villages like Nowy Targ, Zakopane, and Bukowina Tatrzanska. Finding Ella's parents would open a separate branch of European research.

5

Susanna Hooke's Parentage

Frederick W. Todd's book Humphrey Hooke of Bristol and His Family and Descendants in England and America During the Seventeenth Century is available through NEHGS or Godfrey Memorial Library in Middletown, CT. The question is whether Susanna Hooke who married Edward Derby appears as a granddaughter, niece, or some other relation of Humphrey Hooke MP of Bristol.

6

Eleanor Whitney and the Whitney Genealogy (Pierce, 1895)

John Whitney's 1895 genealogy by Frederick Clifton Pierce is free at Internet Archive and FamilySearch Digital Library. The goal is to find the Connecticut branch that produced Eleanor Whitney and trace her marriage to Samuel Shepherd. This also potentially opens a Whitney-line SAR application if Revolutionary War service exists in that branch.

7

Cynthia Salisbury's Parents, Cortland County NY

Cortland, NY 1820 federal census, Cortland County marriage records, and New York state records from 1780 to 1823. If Cynthia's father connects to Captain Abraham Salisbury (Connecticut militia, Lake Champlain, 1777), that is a confirmed SAR patriot and closes the third line the family record names.

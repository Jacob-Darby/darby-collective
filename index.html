Chronological History

800 Years of the Darby Name

c. 1200 — Lincolnshire, England
The Name Appears in Lincolnshire
The earliest recorded Darby family: a pedigree noting that Nicholas Tamworth of Tamworth married Jane, daughter of Darby, of Leverton, Lincolnshire — c. 1200. The Darby name derives from Old Norse djúr-byr (deer settlement), place name for Derby, Derbyshire, recorded in the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle as "Doeraby" in 917 AD.
1346–1620 — Lincolnshire, England
The Darby Hall Family of Leake & Leverton
Ralph de Darby of Bennington (1346), Thomas Darby (1346, 1353), Robert Darby (1357), Roger Darby of Leverton, Ralph Darby of Leake (1517), Thomas Darby of Bennington (1536–1544). The family maintained Darby Hall — a large ancient building in Leake/Leverton, Lincolnshire, "the residence of the family of that name who were considerable proprietors in this and the adjoining parishes for several centuries." Memorials of the Darby and Gilbert families remain in the chancel floor at Leake church. William Darby was living c.1620; Thomas Darby of Leake died 1659.
1623 — London / New England
First Darby in American Colonial Record
William Darby of Dorchester, England petitions the Council for New England, February 18, 1623, that associates might begin a plantation at Cape Ann — which led to the settlement of Naumkeag (now Salem, Massachusetts). William Darby is also listed among Representatives to the General Court in England, October 12, 1629 — "William Darby, John Smith, George Philips, Simon Whitcomb, John Davenport..." This places a Darby at the founding administrative moment of Massachusetts Bay Colony.
1650 — Surrey, England
Agnes Derby's Will Names "Edward Derby, in New England"
Agnes Derby of Bisley, Surrey, England makes a nuncupative will (May 20, 1650, proved June 1, 1650), bequeathing money in trust to Henry Collier "He to pay Edward Derby, in New England, ten pounds when he shall come and demand the same." This is almost certainly the first documentary mention of your direct ancestor Edward¹ Darby — placing him in New England by 1650 and connected to a Surrey/Bisley England family. Bisley is approximately 30 miles southwest of London.
January 25, 1658/59 — Boston, Massachusetts
Edward Derby Marries Susanna Hooke
The Boston vital records confirm: "Edward Derby was married to Susanna Hook, January 25, 1659, by Richard Bellingham, Deputy Governor." Richard Bellingham served as Governor of Massachusetts Bay Colony multiple times — this was a significant civic officiant, suggesting the families had standing in the colony. Edward is also recorded as having settled in Braintree, Massachusetts, where a daughter Mary was born December 29, 1660, and Priscilla, June 20, 1672.
1702 — Dunstable, Massachusetts
James² Darby Born
James² Darby born April 30, 1702 in Dunstable, Essex (now Middlesex), Massachusetts. He later settled at Mortlake Society, Canterbury, Connecticut — one of the most active Separatist/Baptist communities in colonial New England. He married Eleanor Shepherd, connecting the Darby line to the Puritan Great Migration families of Ralph Shepard (London, 1635) and John Whitney (London/Isleworth, 1635).
1731–1813 — Canterbury, CT / Cortland, NY
Eleazor³ Darby — Lives Through the Revolution
Eleazor³ born July 6, 1731 in Canterbury, Connecticut. He was 44–52 years old during the Revolutionary War (1775–1783), living in an actively Patriot Connecticut community. Hampton, Connecticut men marched through the night after hearing of Lexington (April 1775). Eleazor likely rendered some form of patriot service — making him a prime SAR candidate. He died January 31, 1813 in Cortland, New York, aged 81. Buried: Cortland Old Burial Ground.
1796–1806 — Homer, New York
Joseph⁴ Marries Anna Grow — The Grow Connection
Joseph⁴ Darby marries Anna Grow of Hampton, Connecticut in 1796. Anna (b. February 15, 1777) is the daughter of Thomas Grow of Hampton — whose family settled Grow Hill in 1723, came from Ipswich, Massachusetts, and bears a Germanic surname (Grow/Groh). Anna died June 30, 1806, aged 29, leaving three children: Delia, Lyman, and Thomas (your ancestor). Joseph remarried Asenath Tyler in 1807. Their descendants filled Cortland County, NY through the 19th century.
1864 — Cortland, NY → Clarksville, Iowa
Thomas DeWight⁶ Crosses Into Iowa
Thomas DeWight⁶ Darby (b. November 23, 1842, Cortland, NY) marries Frances Elizabeth Bement in 1864 and migrates to Clarksville, Iowa — your family's first Iowa generation. He dies August 30, 1900 and is buried at Lynwood Cemetery, Clarksville. His son Lucius Edwin⁷ (b. 1868) becomes a Plymouth Brethren lay minister and farmer in Clarksville, rooting the family in Butler County, Iowa for generations.
1896–1985 — Clarksville & Nashua, Iowa
Clarence Arthur⁸ — Baptist Farmer of Nashua
Clarence Arthur⁸ (b. July 7, 1896) marries Katie Sutter on May 10, 1917 in Charles City, Iowa. Katie — originally named Katrina Maria — had no birth certificate (born at home) and legally chose "Katie Darby," the name she was always called. Baptist. Farmer. Five children. Died August 28, 1985, Oak Hill Cemetery, Nashua, Iowa.

Pre-Colonial Origins

England & Beyond

Before Massachusetts. Before Connecticut. Before Iowa. The Darby name traces to medieval Lincolnshire, and four distinct English families joined your bloodline between 1635 and 1700.

Darby Hall — Leake & Leverton, Lincolnshire

c. 1200 – c. 1700

A large ancient building called Darby Hall stood as the family seat in Leake (partly Leverton), Lincolnshire for several centuries. The family were "considerable proprietors in this and the adjoining parishes," allied by marriage to the Dymocke family. Ralph Darby (1346), Thomas Darby (1346, 1353), Robert (1357), Roger of Leverton, Ralph of Leake (1517), Thomas of Bennington (1536–1544), William (1571), Thomas (1597–1659), Dymoke Darby (died Leake 1701). Memorials in Leake church chancel floor. The name likely derives from their proximity to the city of Derby, Derbyshire, itself named from Old Norse for "deer settlement."

Stepney & Limehouse, London — Ralph Shepard

1606 – 1635 emigration

Ralph Shepard was born c.1606 in the Limehouse district of Stepney, Middlesex (London) — a neighborhood named for its lime kilns along the Thames. A tailor by profession, he was brought before the Court of High Commissions as a religious nonconformist on April 24, 1634. Faced with persecution, he married Thankslorde Perkins at St. Bride Fleet Street, London (May 21, 1632), then fled England June 30, 1635 aboard the ship Abigail. He was one of the founding settlers of Dedham, Massachusetts, signing the Dedham Covenant. He died 1693 in Malden, Massachusetts, aged c.87.

Isleworth-on-Thames & Bowe Lane, London — John Whitney

c.1589 – 1635 emigration

John Whitney Sr. was born c.1589 and lived first at Isleworth-on-Thames (8 miles upstream from Westminster) where his eldest children were christened. He later moved to Bowe Lane in the City of London, near St. Paul's Cathedral. A member of the Merchant Tailors' Company (London's most powerful trade guild), he enrolled his eldest son at the Merchant Tailors' School in 1631. In April 1635, he registered as a passenger on the ship Elizabeth and Ann, arriving in Watertown, Massachusetts in July 1635. He received 16 acres of homestead and held 198 additional acres by 1642. Town Clerk of Watertown until his death June 1, 1673. Full genealogy: Pierce, 1895 (free at Internet Archive).

Bisley, Surrey, England — Edward Derby

c.1625 – named in 1650 English will

Agnes Derby of Bisley, Surrey, England made a will (May 20, 1650, proved June 1, 1650) bequeathing money in trust to Henry Collier, "He to pay Edward Derby, in New England, ten pounds when he shall come and demand the same." Bisley is in Surrey, approximately 30 miles southwest of London. This is the strongest English document connecting your direct ancestor Edward¹ Darby to a specific English family and location. He is later confirmed in Boston records marrying Susanna Hooke January 25, 1658/59, and settling in Braintree, Massachusetts.

Bristol, Gloucestershire — Humphrey Hooke MP

c.1580s – 1658

Humphrey Hooke of Bristol was a Member of Parliament and prominent Bristol merchant. His son William Hooke (c.1612–1652) emigrated to Salisbury, Massachusetts and became Governor of New Somerseshire (Province of Maine). William died in 1652, buried St. Stephens, Bristol — he had returned to England. Humphrey's will (June 25, 1658) names grandsons William, Jacob, and Josias, calling two of them "the most stubborn and unruly boys." Susanna Hooke who married Edward Derby in Boston five months later (January 1659) is the probable granddaughter or niece. Published: Frederick W. Todd, Humphrey Hooke of Bristol and His Family and Descendants in England and America.

Podhale Region, Southern Poland — Podhaski

c.1880s–1910s immigration to Iowa

Ella Podhaski's surname is a toponymic Polish name meaning "from the Podhale" — the highland plateau at the foot of the Tatra Mountains in southern Poland, near Kraków, in the Małopolska (Lesser Poland) region. Polish immigration to the American Midwest concentrated in the 1880s–1910s, with many settling in industrial Iowa cities. Finding Ella's parents' village of origin in Poland would open a completely separate European genealogy in one of Central Europe's most historically rich regions — the Carpathian highlands where Polish, Slovak, and Ruthenian cultures meet.

A Timeline

Key dates from the earliest English records to Iowa, in chronological order.

  1. Pre-Roman, East Midlands, England
    The River Derwent and the Celtic Place Name
    The earliest layer of the name comes from a Celtic root word for the River Derwent, meaning "valley thick with oaks." The Romans established a fort here called Derventio around 80 AD as part of their road network across the East Midlands. These are the geographic conditions that later produced the name your family carries.
  2. 870s AD, Danelaw, England
    Danish Vikings Name the Settlement: Djura-by
    The Great Heathen Army of Danish Vikings swept through Lincolnshire and the East Midlands in 865 to 878 AD, establishing the Danelaw. They renamed the Roman settlement Djúra-bý, meaning "deer farm" or "deer settlement" in Old Norse. This is the direct etymological source of your surname. The Darby/Derby name is Danish Viking in origin. The settlement became one of the Five Boroughs of the Danelaw.
  3. 917 AD, East Midlands
    Anglo-Saxon Chronicle Records "Deoraby"
    The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle records that Lady Æthelflæd of Mercia captured the borough called Deoraby in July 917, retaking it from the Danes. This is the first written record of the place name that became your surname. The Chronicle notes "there were also killed four thegns who were dear to her inside the gates." The town is recorded as divided by water, with the Viking Deoraby settlement on the north side.
  4. 1066 to 1086, Lincolnshire and Derbyshire
    Norman Conquest: de Ferrers Granted 114 Lincolnshire Manors
    Henry de Ferrers, a Norman lord from Ferrières in Normandy, participated in William the Conqueror's invasion in 1066 and was granted 210 manors, with 114 concentrated in Derbyshire and Lincolnshire, the exact region where your family was living. The 1086 Domesday Book records "Derbei" as the town's name. Your ancestors in Leake and Leverton, Lincolnshire were living within the Ferrers feudal territory at this time, though they are not yet recorded by name.
  5. 1138 to 1160, Lincolnshire and Derbyshire
    Earl of Derby Title Created; First Use of the Surname
    Robert de Ferrers was created the first Earl of Derby in 1138, taking the title from the same Viking town name that is your surname. Then between 1160 and 1182, the first recorded use of "de Derby" as a personal surname appears: Roger de Derby, documented in the Register of Antiquities, Lincolnshire, during the reign of Henry II. This Roger de Derby is recorded in Lincolnshire, the same county as your Darby Hall family. He is almost certainly a direct ancestor or close collateral relative.

    6. Derby = Darby: the same word

    Before 1200, there was no consistent English spelling. Scribes wrote what they heard. The city appears as Deoraby (917), Derbei (1086), and Darbye (1610 maps). The surname appears as both Derby and Darby through the 1600s in the same family's records. In British English, Derby is still pronounced "Darby" today. The two spellings are not two separate names. They are the same name, the same family, written down differently by different hands across seven centuries. Derbyshire simply adds the Old English word for county (scir) and means "person from Derbyshire." Darbyshire is the same thing with the regional spelling. Early American migrants from Darbyshire often dropped the suffix, and some American "Darby" families are shortened Darbyshires.

  6. c. 1200, Lincolnshire, England
    The Name Appears in Lincolnshire
    The earliest Darby record: a pedigree noting that Nicholas Tamworth of Tamworth married Jane, daughter of Darby, of Leverton, Lincolnshire, around 1200. The name comes from Old Norse djur-byr (deer settlement), the place name for Derby, Derbyshire, recorded in the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle as "Doeraby" in 917 AD.
  7. 1346 to 1620, Lincolnshire
    Darby Hall, Leake and Leverton
    The family held Darby Hall, a large property in Leake and Leverton, Lincolnshire, described as "the residence of the family of that name who were considerable proprietors in this and the adjoining parishes for several centuries." Documented members span 1346 (Ralph de Darby of Bennington) through 1701 (Dymoke Darby, died at Leake). Memorials survive in the Leake church chancel floor.
  8. 1623, London and New England
    First Darby in American Colonial Records
    William Darby of Dorchester, England petitioned the Council for New England on February 18, 1623, for a plantation at Cape Ann, which led to the settlement of Naumkeag (now Salem, Massachusetts). He also appears among Representatives to the General Court in England on October 12, 1629, placing a Darby at the founding moment of Massachusetts Bay Colony.
  9. 1650, Surrey, England
    Agnes Derby's Will Names Edward Derby in New England
    Agnes Derby, widow of Augustine Derby of Bisley, Surrey, made a nuncupative will on May 20, 1650, proved June 1, 1650. She left money in trust to Henry Collier of Horsabell, yeoman: "He to pay Edward Derby, in New England, ten pounds when he shall come and demand the same." The conditional phrasing (Edward must actively claim it) suggests a nephew or more distant relation rather than a son; a mother typically would not word it this way. The key lead is Augustine Derby himself. If his baptism, marriage, and children can be found in Bisley St. John the Baptist parish records (indexed in IGI from 1561), his relationship to Edward will become clear. Bisley sits about 30 miles southwest of London.
  10. January 25, 1658/59, Boston
    Edward Derby Settles at Weymouth, Then Boston
    Edward Derby came from Bisley, county Surrey, England, confirmed in the 1909 Genealogical and Family History of the State of Maine. He first settled at Weymouth, Massachusetts, then removed to Boston. Boston vital records: "Edward Derby was married to Susanna Hook, January 25, 1659, by Richard Bellingham, Deputy Governor." He settled in Braintree, Massachusetts, where daughters Mary (born December 29, 1660) and Priscilla (born June 20, 1672) are recorded. He died in 1724, making him approximately 85 to 90 years old, a long life for the period. Since Augustine Derby was also from Bisley, Augustine was almost certainly Edward's older brother or uncle.
  11. June 24, 1675 and March 26, 1676, Swansea MA and Pawtucket River RI
    Two Ancestors Killed in King Philip's War
    Jacob's ancestry includes two confirmed King Philip's War casualties within nine months, on opposite sides of the same conflict. William Salisbury (b. 1622, Llanrhaidr, Wales), ancestor through the Cynthia Salisbury line, was killed June 24, 1675 at Swansea with his son John after John fired on Indian looters of their home. They were the war's first casualties, memorialized on a Massachusetts monument erected 1912. Captain Michael Pierce (b. c.1615, Hingham, Kent, England), ancestor through Mary Pierce who married Martin Salisbury, was killed March 26, 1676 at Pawtucket River in "Pierce's Fight," one of the war's worst disasters. He led 52 colonists and 20 Christian Indians into a Narragansett ambush; nearly his entire force was killed. Both were English immigrants. Both died in the same war. Both appear in the family tree through Cynthia Salisbury who married Lyman⁶ Darby.
  12. 1702, Dunstable, Massachusetts
    James³ Darby Born
    James³ was born April 30, 1702 in Dunstable, Essex (now Middlesex), Massachusetts. He later settled at Mortlake Society in Canterbury, Connecticut, one of the more active Separatist/Baptist communities in colonial New England. He married Eleanor Shepard (b. c.1701), daughter of Samuel Shepard (son of Ralph Shepard, London 1635) and Eleanor Whitney (daughter of John Whitney, Isleworth London 1635). Eleanor Shepard was therefore the granddaughter of BOTH Ralph Shepard and John Whitney, now confirmed in a primary source will document. The Hudson-Mohawk Genealogical Memoirs confirms Eleanor was "daughter of Samuel Shepard and Eleanor Whitney, her first ancestors in this country being Ralph Shepard, John Whitney, Baptist Smedley and Thomas Tarbell." The specific Whitney branch is now confirmed: Eleanor Whitney (b. c.1684) was the daughter of Deacon Joshua Whitney (b. July 15, 1635, Watertown MA; d. Aug 7, 1719; son of John Whitney of Watertown) and his third wife Abigail Tarbell (daughter of Thomas Tarbell of Groton, Massachusetts). Deacon Joshua Whitney's will dated April 17, 1713, proved October 6, 1719 at Groton, names "my five daughters now living, Mary Peirce, Elenor Shepard, Sarah Juell, Martha Williams, and Elisabeth Farnworth", placing Eleanor Whitney Shepard directly in a probate court primary source. Samuel Shepard and Eleanor Whitney lived in Plainfield, Windham County CT. Their daughter Eleanor Shepard (b. March 6, 1701, Plainfield CT) is confirmed by FamilySearch Connecticut Births. The Tarbell mention in the memoirs ("Thomas Tarbell") now resolves as Abigail Tarbell's father, Eleanor Whitney's maternal grandfather. A collateral note: Eli Whitney, inventor of the cotton gin (b. December 8, 1765), descends from John Whitney through a brother of Deacon Joshua, making him a distant cousin of this Darby line. Source: Pierce, Descendants of John Whitney (1895); Will of Deacon Joshua Whitney, Groton MA (1713, proved 1719).
  13. 1731 to 1813, Canterbury, CT and Cortland, NY
  14. 1749 to 1777, Canterbury, Windham County, Connecticut
    Darby Siblings Marry Into the Cleveland Family
    Canterbury town marriage records reveal the full Darby sibling picture: William Darby married Abigail Cleveland (November 9, 1749), Ruth Darby married Samuel Cleveland (May 7, 1751), Jedediah Darby married Lucretia Cleveland (January 2, 1755), and Olive Darby married Daniel Bacon (April 27, 1777). Three siblings marrying into the same Cleveland family within six years shows how tightly bound these Canterbury Baptist families were. Eleazor⁴, born July 6, 1731, was the sibling who eventually led the migration to Cortland, New York.
  15. 1731, Canterbury, Windham County, Connecticut
    Eleazor⁴ Darby Lives Through the Revolution
    Eleazor⁴ was born July 6, 1731 in Canterbury, Connecticut and was 44 to 52 years old during the Revolutionary War. Connecticut was "The Provisions State." He died January 31, 1813 in Cortland, New York, aged 81. Buried at Cortland Old Burial Ground. His Revolutionary War service is an open research question and the most direct path to SAR eligibility.

She died June 30, 1806, aged 29, leaving three children: Delia, Lyman, and Thomas (your ancestor). Joseph remarried Asenath Tyler in 1807.
  17. 1864, Cortland, NY to Clarksville, Iowa
    Thomas DeWight⁷ Moves to Iowa
    Thomas DeWight⁷ (b. November 23, 1842, Cortland, NY) married Frances Elizabeth Bement in 1864 and moved to Clarksville, Butler County, Iowa, the first Darby in Iowa. He died August 30, 1900 and is buried at Lynwood Cemetery, Clarksville, Butler County, Iowa (west edge of town on Highway C33, established 1883). His wife Frances Bement Darby (d. 1920) and their son Lucius + wife Amy Scott Darby are also buried there. Thomas and Frances share the cemetery with Samuel Lenhart + Margaret McMillan (1813-1895) and other Clarksville families. Thomas Darby Sr. (1802-1883) was born in Homer, Cortland County only four months after his family arrived by horse and wagon from Canterbury, CT. His mother Anna Grow died in 1806 when he was four; his father Joseph Darby remarried Asenath Tyler (1779-1852), who raised him. Eleazor⁴ Darby (1731-1813) is buried alongside son Joseph Darby + Anna Grow Darby in a small farm cemetery above the former John Keep County Home in Homer, NY. His son Lucius Edwin⁸ (b. 1868) became a Plymouth Brethren lay minister and farmer there.

The Connected Lines

The Darby name is the spine, but the branches run through Connecticut, London, Bristol, Hampton, the Polish highlands, Germany, and Switzerland. Every name below entered the family through marriage. These are factual findings from active research across each line.

Darby / Derby

Lincolnshire, England to Boston, MA to Iowa (c.1200 to present)
The patriline. Name from Old Norse djur-byr (deer settlement). First recorded c.1200 in Lincolnshire. First American: Edward Derby, Boston, 1650s. Held Darby Hall in Leake and Leverton, Lincolnshire, for several centuries. Coat of arms: argent, a stag's head caboshed gules, attired or, between three roundels azure.

Whitney

Herefordshire to Westminster, London to Watertown MA (c.1045 to present)
Eleanor Whitney descends from John Whitney (bp. July 20, 1592, St. Margaret's Westminster), son of Thomas Whitney of Lambeth Marsh, who married Mary Bray on May 10, 1583. The Whitney family of Herefordshire traces to Turstin the Fleming (c.1045), who arrived with William the Conqueror in 1066 and received lands at Pencomb and Whitney. The family held Whitney Castle at Whitney-on-Wye for centuries, serving as Sheriffs of Herefordshire, Members of Parliament, and Crusade participants. The Whitney coat of arms (azure, cross checkered gold) dates to the Crusade era. John Whitney arrived in Watertown, Massachusetts in July 1635. Motto: Magnanimiter crucem sustine (gallantly uphold the cross).

Shepherd / Shepard

Limehouse, Stepney, London to Dedham MA (1606 to present)
Eleanor Shepherd, wife of James³ Darby, was the daughter of Samuel Shepherd and Eleanor Whitney. The line traces to Deacon Ralph Shepard (b. c.1606, Limehouse, Stepney, London), a Puritan tailor who faced religious persecution and left England June 30, 1635 aboard the ship Abigail. He co-founded Dedham, Massachusetts. His wife, Thankslorde Perkins, married Ralph at St. Bride Fleet Street, London on May 21, 1632 (this record was found in 1992). She signed a petition to the General Court in 1651. His daughter Sarah was baptized at Stepney Parish, Middlesex on August 6, 1633, confirming Ralph's Stepney presence before emigration. His parents are genuinely unknown: the "Isaac Shepard" connection found in many family trees refers to a different man (a goldsmith's son born 1603), and was explicitly rejected by Anderson's Great Migration Study, the authoritative source on this family. Both Ralph's parents and Thankslorde's parents are a confirmed brick wall. Collateral descendant: Eli Whitney, inventor of the cotton gin.

Hooke

Chichester, West Sussex to Bristol, Gloucestershire, England to Salisbury MA (c.1580 to 1659)
Susanna Hooke, wife of Edward¹ Darby, was most probably the daughter of William Hooke (b. April 1612, Bristol; son of Alderman Humphrey Hooke) and his first wife Jane Whalley, making her a granddaughter of Alderman Humphrey Hooke (b. c.1580, Chichester, West Sussex; d. June 25, 1658, Bristol). Humphrey became the pre-eminent Bristol merchant of his era: Sheriff of Bristol 1614, Mayor 1629 and 1643, MP for Bristol in both the Short and Long Parliaments (1640), Lord of seven manors in Gloucestershire and Somerset, and one of the patentees of Newfoundland in 1610. He had 14 children with Cecily Young (married 1605 at Horfield, Gloucester), all buried at St. Stephen's Church, Bristol. His son William Hooke (c.1612-1652) emigrated to New England in 1635, ministered at Taunton and Salisbury, Massachusetts, and was styled "Governor" of New Somerseshire. Humphrey's will dated June 25, 1658 names grandsons William, Jacob, and Josias, calling two of them "the most stubborn and unruly boys." Susanna married Edward Derby in Boston five months later. Frederick W. Todd, Humphrey Hooke of Bristol and His Family and Descendants (1938, free at Internet Archive) covers the full family. Humphrey's own parents are unknown: Todd concluded "nothing here given is conclusive as to Alderman Hooke's ancestry" but suggests possible connection to the Hooke family of Chichester and a "Thomas Hooke, Knight of Windsor" under Henry VIII.

Origins in England

Several branches of this family left documented records in England before emigrating. The Darby name appears in Lincolnshire as early as 1200. The Whitney line, confirmed through the Whitney Research Group, goes further back, to a Norman knight who came with William the Conqueror in 1066. Other lines came through London, Bristol, and Surrey.

Darby Hall, Leake and Leverton, Lincolnshire

Roger de Derby 1160-1182 · Family at Leake/Leverton/Bennington c.1200 to 1701

The definitive published source is Viola A. Derby Bromley, Derby Genealogy (Grafton Press, New York, 1905), which cites Thompson's History and Antiquities of Boston, England. The Lincolnshire Darby family is documented from "about the year 1200." Confirmed members: Ralph de Darby of Bennington (1346), married Alice, daughter of John Read of Wrangle. Thomas (1346, 1353) and Robert (1357) concurrent. Ralph Darby mentioned by Dugdale at Leake 1517. Thomas Darby of Bennington (1536, 1539, 1540, 1544) + Alice, daughter of John Langton (1547). William Darby (son of Thomas, alive 1571). William Darby (son of William, alive c.1620). Thomas Darby d. Leake 1659. Dymoke Darby d. Leake 1701. Memorials to the Darby and Gilbert families survive in the Leake church chancel floor. Darby Hall itself, partly in Leake and partly in Leverton, is the ancient family seat. Arms confirmed in Burke's General Armory: Argent, a chevron sable between three garbs sable, identical to the arms displayed on this site. The same 1905 genealogy explicitly states: "The antecedents in England of Thomas Derby, of Stow, Massachusetts, have never been ascertained", meaning the primary-source connection between Edward¹ Derby (from Bisley, Surrey) and this Lincolnshire family remains unconfirmed, despite the matching arms. The most plausible theory: Augustine Derby of Bisley was a younger son of the c.1620 William Darby who moved south to Surrey.

Ralph Shepard, Stepney and Limehouse, London

1606 to 1635 emigration

Ralph Shepard was born c.1606 in the Limehouse district of Stepney, Middlesex, a neighborhood named for its lime kilns along the Thames. A tailor by profession, he was brought before the Court of High Commissions as a religious nonconformist in April 1634. He married Thankslorde Perkins at St. Bride Fleet Street, London on May 21, 1632, then left England June 30, 1635 aboard the ship Abigail. He helped found Dedham, Massachusetts, signing the Dedham Covenant. He died 1693 in Malden, Massachusetts, aged about 87.

John Whitney, Isleworth-on-Thames and Bowe Lane, London

c.1589 to 1635 emigration

John Whitney Sr. was born c.1589 and lived at Isleworth-on-Thames, 8 miles upstream from Westminster, where his eldest children were christened. He later lived on Bowe Lane in the City of London, near St. Paul's Cathedral. A member of the Merchant Tailors' Company, he sailed in April 1635 and arrived in Watertown, Massachusetts in July 1635 on the ship Elizabeth and Ann. He was granted 16 acres on arrival and 198 more by 1642. Town Clerk of Watertown until his death June 1, 1673.

Family Arms

The armigerous Darby family of Benington, Lincolnshire, England. Benington is less than 20 miles from Darby Hall at Leake, where the family held land from at least 1200 AD.

What the shield means
Silver background (Argent)
The field of the shield is silver, the heraldic term for which is argent. Silver signifies sincerity, peace, and the enduring nature of the family line.
Black inverted V (Chevron sable)
The large inverted-V shape crossing the middle of the shield is a chevron, painted black (sable). The chevron is one of the oldest heraldic ordinaries. It signifies protection and the upholding of good conscience against adversity; a mark of those who defend and build.
Three black wheat sheaves with gold bands (Three garbs sable, banded or)
Surrounding the chevron are three garbs, the heraldic word for bound sheaves of wheat. They are black (sable) with a gold (or) tie around each bundle. One appears above the chevron; two appear below. Wheat sheaves were the natural charge of Lincolnshire agricultural gentry. The three garbs represent abundance from honest labor, the sustaining of a family through the land.
What sits above the shield
The crest: A red antelope's head, cut cleanly at the neck (erased gules), with gold mane, horn tufts, and antlers (maned, tufted, armed, and attired or). The antelope in heraldry represents the union of power and gentleness, fierce in defense, peaceful by nature. The red color (gules) signals martial courage.
The family motto
"Ut cunque placuerit Deo"
"Howsoever it shall have pleased God"
A declaration of submission to Providence. Whatever outcome God wills, the family accepts. It is not fatalism but faith: the same spirit that sustained the family through King Philip's War, the frontier, and two centuries of American life.

Sources: Barbour Collection New London Vol. 29; Clarence Beebe, Monograph of the Descent of the Family of Beebe (1904), p. 33 and p. 47; Lynda Darby Ozinga, Tompkins County NYGenWeb.

Jeduthan Beebe's Revolutionary War Service: Third SAR Path

The Beebe-Peck-Darby chain is now confirmed in four primary sources. Jeduthan Beebe (b. September 10, 1734, New London CT; son of William Beebe Jr. b. Jan 15, 1704 New London, and Jerusha Rogers) married Lucretia Daton on December 24, 1753 at New London, confirmed by the New London Church Record: "Jeduthan Beebee son of William Beebee of New London and Lucretia Daton daughter of Ephraim Daton of said New London dec'd were joyned in marriage covenant December 24 AD 1753." Civil patriotic service (Committee of Safety, loyalty oath, supply) also qualifies. Confirming Jeduthan's service opens a third documented SAR path, the best-documented chain of all three.

Augustine Derby in Bisley Surrey: Edward's English Family (and Possible Lincolnshire Connection)

Agnes Derby's 1650 will names her as widow of Augustine Derby of Bisley, Surrey. The conditional bequest ("when he shall come and demand the same") suggests Edward was a nephew or more distant relative, not Agnes's son. The next step: search St. John the Baptist Bisley parish registers on FamilySearch (IGI-indexed from 1561) for Augustine Derby's baptism, marriage, and children. Also search "Henry Collier of Horsabell" (Horsabell is a specific Bisley-area place and Collier may be traceable in local Surrey records). The St John the Baptist, Bisley register runs from 1561 and is fully indexed in the IGI on FamilySearch, baptisms 1561-1812, marriages 1561-1754, burials 1561-1865. Searching "Derby" in those records c.1580-1650 should find Augustine Derby's marriage, possibly his own baptism, and Edward Derby's baptism if he was born in Bisley. The Archdeaconry Court of Surrey Wills (1660-1858) and Bishop's Commissary of Winchester Wills (1507-1858) hold relevant probate records; Agnes Derby's 1650 will may be in the Prerogative Court of Canterbury Commonwealth Probates Vol. 1, 1650-1651 (Matthews volume 5), accessible through Findmypast. One unverified genealogy.com family tree asserts "Father of Edward Derby I was Augustine Derby, born in Bisley, County Surrey, Eng. and died Bef. 1650", not confirmed in primary sources but consistent with Agnes's will. The critical open question is whether Augustine Derby of Bisley was a younger son of the documented Lincolnshire Darby family (William Darby alive c.1620 is the last confirmed Lincolnshire member before Edward's arrival in New England). The matching coat of arms, argent, a chevron between three garbs, sable, between the Lincolnshire family and the arms claimed by Edward¹'s line is the strongest circumstantial evidence.

Eleazor⁴ Darby's Revolutionary War Service

Eleazor⁴ (b. July 6, 1731, Canterbury, CT; wife Anna Doubleday, whose surname was preserved in his great-grandson Wayland Doubleday Darby; d. January 31, 1813, Cortland, NY) was 44 to 52 during the Revolution in Windham County, "The Provisions State." A CT Archives index entry previously flagged as potentially his, "Eleazer (A) account for entertaining Nicholas & Mark Foquett, Sept. 1778, Vol. XIV: 164,165", is actually Eleazer Cady of Windham County, not Darby. The Cady surname section in the index contains all surrounding entries (Abijah, Abner, Amos, Asa, Asahel from Canterbury and nearby towns). The CT SAR Patriot Index D to J (55 pages, scanned July 2026) contains no Darby or Derby entry from Canterbury or Windham County. The only "Derby" in the entire index is John Derby (b. February 1, 1750 CT, Soldier 4th CT Regiment), a different family entirely. This means no SAR application has yet been filed using Eleazor⁴ as the patriot ancestor. It is not proof of non-service, the index only covers patriots already claimed by SAR members. The definitive check remains the 37-volume CT Archives collection at the CT State Library, specifically the Darby and Derby entries in that index. This search has not been completed. Note: separately, the "Records of Revolutionary soldiers of Cortland County, New York state: with unpublished genealogical notes" (FamilySearch Library) may contain Eleazor Darby, as he died in Homer in 1813 having lived there since 1802. Confirming the Darby-Salisbury Link in Cortland County Records

Is Your Surname a Variant?

These surname variants all share the same Viking root. If your family uses one of these spellings, you may share a common geographic origin even if not a direct genealogical connection.

Darby / Derby
Same word, different spelling. The city of Derby is still pronounced "Darby" in England. Interchangeable in colonial American records through the 1700s.
Derbyshire / Darbyshire
Means "person from Derbyshire." Most common in Lancashire (West Derby area), not the county itself. Some American "Darby" families are shortened Darbyshires. A Massachusetts man was recorded as Darby, Darbe, Darbeyshier, and Derbyshire across different documents 1672 to 1725.
O'Darby / Irish Darby
In Ireland, Darby is an anglicization of Gaelic O Diarmada (descendant of Diarmaid). Phonetically similar but etymologically unrelated to the English surname. Separate genealogical line.
de Derby (Norman spelling)
The "de" prefix indicates someone from Derby. Roger de Derby (Lincolnshire, 1160 to 1182) is the earliest recorded use of the surname. The Lincolnshire Darby Hall family stopped using the "de" prefix as English spelling conventions changed in the 1300s.
